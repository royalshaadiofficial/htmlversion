Relationship Tips for Couples in the First Year of Marriage

✦ Royal Shaadi Editorial Team · June 26, 2026 · 11 min read

The wedding is over, the lehenga is packed away, and the two of you are finally just... married. The first year is when the real relationship begins — two people, two families, and a hundred small adjustments, all happening at once. It's normal for it to feel exciting one week and exhausting the next. Here are 8 honest, practical tips for navigating your first year of marriage — the part nobody puts on the wedding card.

01

Talk Before It Becomes a Fight

Communication

A five-minute check-in most evenings does more for a marriage than one long talk every few months.

Communication Daily Check-ins Conflict

Most newly married couples don't fight because they disagree — they fight because something small went unsaid for two weeks and finally boiled over. You're learning a person's moods, habits, and triggers in real time, often while also juggling new in-laws, a new home, or a new city. The single biggest shift that helps here is small and unglamorous: talk about the little things before they pile up.

This doesn't mean turning every evening into a "relationship discussion." It means saying "I didn't love how that went earlier, can we talk about it?" on the same day, instead of two weeks later when it has grown into something bigger than it ever needed to be. Couples who do this report far fewer big blow-up fights — not because they disagree less, but because they deal with friction while it's still small.

"In the first year, most fights aren't really about the dishes or the in-laws — they're about feeling unheard for too long."

It also helps to agree, early on, on how you'll fight — not whether you will, because you will. Will you take a 20-minute break if things get heated? Will you avoid bringing up old arguments? Will you agree never to go to bed on a full-blown fight without at least saying goodnight? These small ground rules, set when you're calm, save you when you're not.

💡

Try This: Pick a fixed time each day — even just 10 minutes after dinner — to talk with no phones around. Not about logistics. Just "how was your day, really?"

02

Build Your Own Rhythm as a Couple

Identity & Routine

Small shared rituals — Sunday breakfast, a weekday walk — quietly become the backbone of a marriage.

Routines Shared Habits Newlywed Life

Before marriage, you each had your own routines — your own way of spending a Sunday, your own bedtime, your own idea of a "normal" weeknight. Now you're building a new, shared rhythm from scratch, and it rarely happens automatically. Couples who settle into married life smoothly tend to be deliberate about creating a few rituals that are entirely theirs.

It can be as simple as always having chai together before either of you checks your phone in the morning, or a Friday night that's reserved — no matter how tired either of you is — for just the two of you. These rituals don't need to be elaborate or Instagram-worthy. Their value lies in repetition: they become the quiet proof, week after week, that you're a team now.

  • Pick one daily ritual (even 10 minutes) that's just for the two of you, no phones.
  • Pick one weekly ritual — a meal, a walk, a show — that becomes "your thing."
  • Revisit your routines every few months; what worked at month one may need tweaking by month eight.
💡

Try This: Ask each other, honestly, "what part of married life still feels unfamiliar?" Most people are quietly adjusting more than they let on — saying it out loud helps.

03

Talk About Money Early — and Often

Finances

Money conversations feel awkward at first, but couples who have them early avoid much bigger arguments later.

Money Budgeting Joint Goals

Money is one of the most common sources of friction in a newly married household, mainly because most couples never actually sat down and discussed it before the wedding. Who pays for what? Do you combine finances or keep them separate, or do a bit of both? What does each of you consider a "big" expense that needs discussion first? These aren't romantic questions, but avoiding them doesn't make the disagreements go away — it just delays them.

#1

3

What works for most couples is a simple structure rather than total merging or total separation: a joint account for shared expenses like rent, groceries, and bills, alongside personal accounts each of you controls without explanation. This way neither partner feels controlled, but big household decisions are still made together.

💡

Try This: Set one "money date" a month — 20 minutes, no judgment — to look at spending together and talk about any upcoming big purchases.

04

Set Gentle Boundaries With In-Laws

Family Dynamics

Healthy boundaries with extended family don't mean distance — they mean clarity.

In-Laws Boundaries Joint Family

For most Indian couples, marriage doesn't just join two people — it joins two families, often with very different expectations about involvement, visits, and decision-making. This is rarely talked about honestly before the wedding, which means a lot of first-year stress comes not from the marriage itself but from figuring out where the family fits in.

The healthiest approach isn't to shut family out, but to present a united front as a couple. Decisions about your own household — your finances, your personal time, your weekends — are best made together first, and then communicated to parents as a joint decision rather than one partner "checking" with the other in front of everyone. This single shift changes how boundary-setting is received on both sides.

"The goal isn't fewer phone calls with family — it's making sure your spouse never has to wonder whose side you're on."

It also helps enormously if each partner takes the lead on managing their own parents' expectations, rather than leaving that conversation to the other person. A husband gently setting boundaries with his own mother lands very differently than a wife being asked to do it — and the same is true in reverse.

💡

Try This: Before a tricky family conversation, agree privately on what you both want the outcome to be — so you walk in already aligned.

05

Keep Making Time for Intimacy

Closeness & Connection

Physical and emotional closeness both need ongoing attention — neither runs on autopilot.

Intimacy Connection Affection

It's easy to assume that closeness — physical and emotional — will simply continue at the same intensity it had during courtship or the early honeymoon phase. In reality, work stress, family obligations, and the sheer logistics of running a household together can quietly crowd out intimacy if you're not paying attention. This is completely normal, but it's worth naming rather than ignoring.

Emotional intimacy and physical intimacy tend to move together — when one partner feels unheard or distant during the day, closeness at night often follows that same distance. So the most useful thing you can do isn't a "fix" for intimacy specifically, but simply protecting the small moments of affection throughout the day: a hand on the shoulder, a hug at the door, actually putting the phone down when your partner is talking.

  • Non-sexual touch — a hug, holding hands, sitting close — matters daily, not just at night.
  • If something feels off, say so kindly rather than withdrawing silently; silence is usually read as rejection.
  • Protect at least one unhurried evening a week where neither of you is exhausted or rushing somewhere.
💡

Try This: If intimacy has felt distant for a while and talking about it feels hard, that's worth raising gently with each other directly — and a couples counsellor can help if it continues to feel stuck.

06

Divide Household Responsibilities Fairly

Daily Life

Fair doesn't always mean equal — it means both partners feel the load is reasonable.

Household Chores Fairness

Running a household together is, for many couples, the first genuinely shared project of the marriage — and it's where a lot of quiet resentment can build if responsibilities default to old patterns or unspoken assumptions rather than an actual conversation. "Who does what" deserves to be discussed out loud, not assumed based on who's "better at it" or what either of your parents did.

A useful exercise is simply listing out everything that needs to happen in a week — cooking, cleaning, groceries, bills, planning, maintenance, even remembering birthdays and appointments — and then honestly discussing who's currently doing what. Often, one partner is carrying a heavier invisible load (the remembering and planning, not just the doing) without either of you having noticed.

💡

Try This: Revisit the division of chores every couple of months — work schedules and energy levels shift, and what felt fair in month two might not feel fair by month seven.

07

Protect Each Other's Individuality

Personal Space

A strong marriage still has room for two separate people with their own friendships and interests.

Independence Friendships Personal Time

It's tempting, especially in the glow of a new marriage, to want to do everything together and merge your social lives completely. But couples who hold onto their own friendships, hobbies, and occasional solo time tend to bring more — not less — into the relationship. A person who's still nurturing their own interests usually has more to talk about, more energy, and less quiet frustration than one who has quietly given everything up.

This is especially worth saying for women, who in many Indian households face more pressure to fold their routines, friendships, and even careers around their new family — often without anyone intending it that way. A supportive spouse actively makes room for their partner's individual life to continue, rather than assuming it will simply happen on its own.

"A good marriage doesn't ask two people to disappear into one — it gives both of them room to stay fully themselves."
💡

Try This: Each of you name one thing — a hobby, a friend group, a class — you want to actively keep or restart this year, and support each other in making time for it.

08

Don't Be Afraid to Ask for Help

Support & Counselling

Seeking support early — from a counsellor, a trusted friend, or each other — is a sign of strength, not failure.

Counselling Mental Health Support Systems

There's still some hesitation, in many Indian families, around the idea of couples counselling — as though needing outside support means the marriage is failing. In reality, the couples who do best long-term are often the ones who normalise asking for help early, before small issues calcify into bigger ones. A few sessions with a counsellor in your first year isn't a red flag — it's simply two people learning a skill, the same way you'd take a class for anything else important.

Support doesn't have to mean formal therapy either. It can mean leaning on an older married couple you both trust, talking honestly with a sibling or close friend instead of bottling things up, or simply being willing to say to your partner, "I think we need to talk to someone about this" without it feeling like an accusation.

💡

Try This: Agree, while things are calm, that either of you can suggest counselling at any point without it being treated as a crisis — just a tool you're both open to using.

The Bigger Picture

None of these tips are about getting marriage "right" from day one — nobody does. The first year is genuinely a learning curve, and the couples who come out the other side closer aren't the ones who avoided every disagreement. They're the ones who kept talking, kept adjusting, and treated each rough patch as something to solve together rather than something to win.

Be patient with each other, and with yourselves. The version of "us" you're building this year is just the first draft — and you'll keep rewriting it, together, for a long time to come.
